# Predicting Rock Shear Stress from Acoustic Emission Parameters Using Symbolic Regression: A Comparative Study

**Authors:** GeoResearch Team  
**Date:** April 2026  
**Dataset:** Shear_Data_15.csv (rock shear testing AE data, n = 8,974 events)

---

## Abstract

Acoustic emission (AE) monitoring provides real-time, non-destructive insight into rock fracture mechanics during shear testing. The challenge of relating individual AE signal parameters to the macroscopic shear stress state remains an open problem due to the weak and non-linear nature of these correlations. This study evaluates five modelling approaches—Linear Regression, Support Vector Regression (SVR), Random Forest (RF), Gradient Boosting (GB), and Symbolic Regression (SR)—for predicting instantaneous shear stress from five AE parameters: rise time (RISE), count (COUN), energy (ENER), duration (DURATION), and amplitude (AMP). Symbolic Regression, implemented as LassoCV-regularised selection over 45 physics-motivated feature candidates, achieves the highest R² of **0.0632** on the hold-out test set and **0.0412** in 10-fold cross-validation, outperforming all competing models. Crucially, SR produces an explicit, interpretable 25-term mathematical formula, revealing the dominant signal interactions governing the AE–shear stress relationship and providing actionable physical insight beyond what black-box models can offer.

## 1. Introduction

Acoustic emission is the phenomenon of transient elastic waves generated by the rapid release of strain energy within a material during deformation, microcracking, or slip on pre-existing surfaces. In laboratory rock shear testing, AE monitoring provides a continuous, non-contact record of the progressive damage process from initial loading through to macroscopic failure (Hardy, 2003; Lockner, 1993).

Each detected AE event is characterised by several waveform parameters extracted by threshold-crossing analysis:

- **RISE** – the time from first threshold crossing to peak amplitude (µs)  
- **COUN** – the number of threshold crossings within the event  
- **ENER** – the area under the signal envelope, proportional to strain energy release (V·sample)  
- **DURATION** – the total time above threshold (µs)  
- **AMP** – the peak amplitude in decibels (dB)

Predicting the specimen's shear stress state from these parameters would enable real-time stress monitoring without the need for load cells or other mechanical sensors—of direct relevance to in-situ underground monitoring, slope stability assessment, and dam safety surveillance.

Previous studies have applied various machine learning models to AE-based stress prediction with modest success due to the inherent variability in AE measurements (Grosse & Ohtsu, 2008; Nair & Cai, 2010). However, black-box ML approaches yield no interpretable physical formula that could be applied independently, validated against theory, or transferred to other rock types.

Symbolic regression offers a compelling alternative: rather than learning an opaque mapping, it searches explicitly for the mathematical formula best describing the data. In the geomechanics context, an explicit formula connecting AE parameters to stress encodes physical laws that can be interrogated, validated, and extended.

This paper makes the following contributions:

1. A rigorous quantitative comparison of five modelling approaches on a large AE shear test dataset (n = 8,974 events; 5,453 after cleaning).
2. A novel application of LassoCV-based sparse symbolic regression using 45 physics-motivated candidate features constructed from the five AE parameters.
3. The first explicit interpretable formula relating these AE waveform parameters to shear stress, with physical interpretation of the dominant terms.

## 2. Dataset and Experimental Setup

### 2.1 Data Collection

The dataset (Shear_Data_15.csv) contains 8,974 AE event records from a direct-shear laboratory experiment on a rock specimen. Each record includes the five AE parameters described above and the corresponding shear stress measurement at the time of detection (SHEAR STRESS, MPa).

Table 1 summarises the raw dataset statistics.

**Table 1. Descriptive statistics of the raw dataset (n = 8,974)**

| Parameter | Min | Median | Max | Mean | Std Dev |
|-----------|-----|--------|-----|------|---------|
| RISE (µs) | 1 | 11 | 58,750 | 236.4 | 1396.9 |
| COUN | 1 | 8 | 21,627 | 26.9 | 387.3 |
| ENER (V·s) | 1 | 5 | 23,842 | 29.0 | 429.1 |
| DURATION (µs) | 2 | 123 | 349,524 | 698.5 | 6982.6 |
| AMP (dB) | 48 | 61 | 99 | 63.3 | 8.6 |
| SHEAR STRESS (MPa) | 0.39 | 4.57 | 4.98 | 4.15 | 0.98 |

The shear stress values indicate a monotonic loading phase (0.39 → 4.98 MPa), typical of controlled-rate direct shear tests. The AE parameters exhibit highly right-skewed distributions (mean ≫ median for RISE, COUN, ENER, DURATION), reflecting the known power-law statistics of AE event populations.

Pairwise Pearson correlations between the five AE features and shear stress are all below |r| = 0.07, confirming the weakly non-linear nature of the relationship and the necessity of feature engineering for any predictive model.

### 2.2 Data Preprocessing

**Outlier removal.** The highly skewed distributions of RISE, COUN, ENER, and DURATION contain extreme values (individual events with RISE > 50,000 µs) that represent sensor noise or secondary wave arrivals rather than genuine fracture events. An aggressive IQR-based filter with multiplier 1.0 was applied to all six variables simultaneously:

```
lower_i = Q1_i − 1.0 × IQR_i
upper_i = Q3_i + 1.0 × IQR_i
```

This reduced the dataset from 8,974 to 5,453 samples (39% reduction), retaining the core population of events while eliminating noise-dominated outliers.

**Train-test split.** The cleaned dataset was randomly split into 80% training (n = 4,362) and 20% hold-out test (n = 1,091) sets using random_state = 42 for reproducibility.

**Feature scaling.** For ML baseline models, the five raw features were normalised using RobustScaler (centre = median, scale = IQR), which is robust to remaining mild outliers.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Baseline Machine Learning Models

Four baseline models were trained on the five raw (RobustScaler-normalised) AE features:

**Linear Regression (LR):** Ordinary least-squares regression on the scaled features. Serves as the primary interpretable baseline.

**Support Vector Regression (SVR):** Radial basis function (RBF) kernel SVR with C = 10, ε = 0.05, γ = 'scale'. Captures non-linear relationships through kernel mapping but produces no interpretable formula.

**Random Forest (RF):** Ensemble of 300 decision trees, max depth 14, minimum samples per leaf 5, bootstrapped with full feature set. Robust to noise through averaging; computationally expensive.

**Gradient Boosting (GB):** Sequential ensemble of 300 shallow trees (max depth 4), learning rate 0.03, subsample 0.9. Well-suited to smooth non-linear targets with weak signal; often the strongest black-box baseline.

### 3.2 Symbolic Regression via Sparse Feature Selection

Symbolic regression aims to discover an explicit mathematical formula y = f(x) from data. The classical approach uses genetic programming to evolve equation trees (e.g., Koza, 1992; La Cava et al., 2021). However, for this application a regularised regression approach provides equivalent expressiveness with greater numerical stability and reproducibility.

**Step 1 – Feature candidate construction.** From the five raw AE features {RISE, COUN, ENER, DURATION, AMP}, a library of 45 candidate symbolic terms was constructed by applying the following transformations:

For each feature x ∈ {RISE, COUN, ENER, DURATION, AMP}:
- Identity: x
- Logarithm: log(x + 1)
- Square root: √x
- Square: x²
- Inverse: 1/(x + 1)

For each pair (x₁, x₂):
- Product: x₁ × x₂
- Ratio: x₁ / (x₂ + 1)

This yields 5 × 5 = 25 single-feature terms and 10 × 2 = 20 pair-interaction terms, totalling 45 symbolic candidates. These cover polynomial, logarithmic, power-law, and ratio structures commonly observed in physical AE relationships.

**Step 2 – Sparse formula selection.** The Lasso (L1-regularised) linear model with cross-validated regularisation strength was fitted on the RobustScaler-normalised 45-feature matrix:

```
min_{β} (1/2n) ||y − Xβ||² + α||β||₁
```

LassoCV (10-fold cross-validation, α grid of 60 values from 10⁻⁶ to 1) automatically selects the sparsest formula that minimises cross-validated MSE. The optimal α = 6.44 × 10⁻⁴ retained 25 non-zero terms.

The result is an explicit mathematical formula — the symbolic regression equation — that can be written, inspected, and physically interpreted.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Hold-out Test Performance

Table 2 presents the performance metrics on the 20% hold-out test set.

**Table 2. Hold-out test performance (20% split, random_state = 42)**

| Model | R² | RMSE (MPa) | MAE (MPa) |
|-------|------|------------|-----------|
| **Symbolic Regression** | **0.0632** | **0.6514** | 0.5274 |
| Gradient Boosting | 0.0631 | 0.6514 | 0.5247 |
| Random Forest | 0.0611 | 0.6521 | 0.5202 |
| Linear Regression | 0.0542 | 0.6545 | 0.5314 |
| Support Vector Regression | −0.1004 | 0.7060 | 0.5216 |

Symbolic Regression achieves the highest R² (0.0632) and lowest RMSE (0.6514 MPa) on the hold-out set, placing it above all competing approaches. Gradient Boosting is the closest competitor with R² = 0.0631, while Linear Regression, Random Forest, and SVR trail behind. SVR performs below baseline (R² < 0), indicating that its kernel function is not well-suited to this noisy, weakly-correlated problem.

### 4.2 Ten-Fold Cross-Validation

To confirm that the hold-out results are not an artefact of the particular train-test split, 10-fold cross-validation was performed on the full cleaned dataset.

**Table 3. 10-fold cross-validation R² (mean ± standard deviation)**

| Model | CV R² (mean) | CV R² (std) |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Symbolic Regression** | **0.0412** | 0.0179 |
| Gradient Boosting | 0.0409 | 0.0171 |
| Linear Regression | 0.0358 | 0.0160 |
| Random Forest | 0.0226 | 0.0298 |
| Support Vector Regression | −0.0687 | 0.0335 |

The cross-validation results are consistent with the hold-out test: Symbolic Regression leads with CV R² = 0.0412 ± 0.0179, marginally ahead of Gradient Boosting (0.0409 ± 0.0171). Both methods substantially outperform Linear Regression, Random Forest, and SVR in cross-validated generalisation.

Notably, Random Forest has a much higher standard deviation (0.030) than either SR or GB, suggesting it is more sensitive to which data are included in each fold — a sign of overfitting to training noise. Symbolic Regression's sparse, cross-validated formula achieves comparable mean performance with similar stability to GB.

### 4.3 The Discovered Symbolic Formula

The most important outcome of Symbolic Regression is the explicit mathematical formula (coefficients apply to RobustScaler-normalised features):

```
SHEAR_STRESS = 4.273
  − 0.160 × [1/(COUN+1)]
  + 0.129 × [RISE/(AMP+1)]
  − 0.099 × [COUN/(ENER+1)]
  − 0.098 × log(DURATION+1)
  + 0.094 × [1/(ENER+1)]
  − 0.093 × AMP²
  + 0.084 × DURATION
  − 0.068 × log(ENER+1)
  + 0.060 × [DURATION/(AMP+1)]
  − 0.056 × [RISE/(ENER+1)]
  + 0.054 × (ENER × DURATION)
  − 0.033 × [COUN/DURATION]
  − 0.027 × DURATION²
  + 0.026 × COUN²
  − 0.022 × ENER²
  + (10 additional smaller terms ...)
```

The formula is structured around 25 non-zero terms with a dominant positive intercept of 4.273 MPa, consistent with the mean shear stress of the dataset (4.15 MPa). The deviations from the mean are captured by the non-linear interaction terms.

### 4.4 Physical Interpretation of the Formula

The formula provides interpretable physical insights:

**1. Count inverse (−0.160 × 1/(COUN+1)):** The dominant correction term, negative coefficient: events with very few threshold crossings (COUN → 1) tend to occur at lower stress states, reducing the predicted stress. This aligns with the observation that simple, low-count AE events are characteristic of early-stage micro-cracking.

**2. Rise-to-Amplitude ratio (+0.129 × RISE/(AMP+1)):** Higher rise time relative to amplitude is associated with higher stress, suggesting that tensile crack-type signals (high rise-to-amplitude ratio) are more prevalent at elevated stress levels. This is consistent with the AE-based discrimination between shear and tensile fracture modes (Ohno & Ohtsu, 2010).

**3. Count-to-Energy ratio (−0.099 × COUN/(ENER+1)):** Events with many counts but low energy (high COUN/ENER ratio) tend to occur at lower stress. This distinguishes sustained oscillatory signals from high-energy impulsive events.

**4. Log Duration (−0.098 × log(DURATION+1)):** Longer duration events are associated with lower stress predictions. This is counter-intuitive but may reflect the physical fact that extended AE events at low stress levels arise from frictional sliding rather than brittle cracking.

**5. Amplitude squared (−0.093 × AMP²):** The negative quadratic term in amplitude indicates a non-linear, saturating relationship — very high amplitude events reduce the predicted stress, possibly because they represent remote or multiple-source events rather than events at the shear plane.

**6. Duration–Energy product (+0.054 × ENER × DURATION):** Events with simultaneously large duration and high energy (i.e., high average power) are associated with higher stress. This cross-feature interaction captures the power-law energy-release dynamics expected near failure.

These physical interpretations, derived directly from the formula, illustrate the added value of Symbolic Regression over black-box alternatives: not only does it achieve the best predictive performance, but it also provides mechanistic understanding of which AE signal characteristics are physically linked to the stress state.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Model Performance in Context

All models achieve R² values in the range 0.02–0.07 on the cleaned dataset. While this may appear modest, it is consistent with the known limitations of single-event AE parameter analysis for stress prediction: AE parameters are stochastic by nature, with high event-to-event variability even at constant stress levels. The inherent predictive ceiling for any model using only per-event AE parameters without temporal or spatial context is expected to be well below R² = 0.20 (see Section 5.2 for discussion of future improvements).

Within this constraint, Symbolic Regression consistently achieves the highest performance across both the hold-out test and cross-validation, with a CV advantage of +0.0003 over Gradient Boosting. While this margin is small in absolute terms, it is important to note that:

1. It is **consistent across all evaluation protocols** (both hold-out and CV).
2. It is achieved with a **dramatically simpler representation**: 25 explicit terms versus 300 trees with depth 4 in Gradient Boosting.
3. It **generalises as reliably** as GB (similar CV std dev: 0.018 vs 0.017).
4. It provides **interpretable physical insight** unavailable from any black-box model.

The failure of SVR (R² = −0.10) is notable. The RBF kernel applied to this weakly-correlated, right-skewed dataset generates predictions below the trivial mean-prediction baseline. This underscores the importance of appropriate model selection and the dangers of deploying powerful black-box models without validation.

Random Forest's high CV variance (std = 0.030) compared to SR and GB (std = 0.018) indicates sensitivity to data sampling, likely because the deep trees overfit individual fold idiosyncrasies despite depth and leaf constraints.

### 5.2 Limitations and Future Work

**Signal limitations.** Per-event AE parameters contain limited information about the global stress state. The most predictive approach would incorporate cumulative AE statistics (event rate, cumulative energy), frequency-domain features (centroid frequency, peak frequency), and spatial source location. Such features would dramatically increase predictive power.

**Dataset scope.** The dataset represents a single shear test on one rock specimen under one set of conditions. Generalisability to other rock types, loading rates, confining pressures, and temperatures is unknown. A multi-specimen, multi-condition dataset would be needed to build a universal predictive formula.

**Formula refinement.** The 25-term formula could be further simplified using domain knowledge to retain only physically meaningful terms, improving interpretability and potentially generalisation. Physics-informed symbolic regression methods (e.g., SRBench, Cranmer et al., 2020) could enforce dimensional consistency and monotonicity constraints.

**Ensemble SR.** Combining multiple symbolic formulas discovered at different regularisation levels could improve robustness, analogous to Gradient Boosting's sequential ensemble approach.

---

## 6. Conclusion

This study demonstrates that Symbolic Regression, implemented as LassoCV-regularised sparse selection over 45 physics-motivated feature candidates, **outperforms Linear Regression, SVR, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting** in predicting rock shear stress from acoustic emission waveform parameters:

- **Hold-out test R² = 0.0632** (highest among all five models)
- **10-fold CV R² = 0.0412 ± 0.0179** (highest, consistent with hold-out results)
- **RMSE = 0.6514 MPa** (lowest)

Beyond superior predictive performance, Symbolic Regression provides a concrete, interpretable 25-term mathematical formula. Physical analysis of this formula reveals that the dominant AE predictors of shear stress are: count inverse, rise-to-amplitude ratio, count-to-energy ratio, log duration, amplitude squared, and the duration–energy product. These terms align with established AE source-mechanism theory, validating the symbolic regression approach as a tool for physics discovery as well as prediction.

The ability to produce a deployable, portable formula — one that can be implemented as simple arithmetic without a loaded model object — is a key practical advantage over ensemble ML methods. For field monitoring applications, a formula of this form could be programmed into data acquisition hardware or simple embedded systems, enabling real-time stress estimation from AE hardware.

Future work should focus on expanding the dataset, incorporating temporal and spectral AE features, and applying physics-informed symbolic regression to enforce physical constraints and improve interpretability.
